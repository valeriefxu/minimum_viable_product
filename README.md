Hypothesis: Extralegal violence in the United States between 1849 and 1865 was not uniform, but structured by region, with racial and slavery-related violence constituting a significantly larger share of incidents in the South/Border South than in the West/North.
Technical Feasibility:
I would like to preface that my MVP is primarily a compartive pipeline. I mainly focused on dividing incidents into South/Border South and West/North regions and calculating the percentage of racial/slavery-related versus other forms of violence in each. However, I wanted to challenge myself and also attempt to implement the other models since its a MVP. I extended my analysis with a probabilistic component that estimates the likelihood of an incident being racial based on variables such as region, state, and target classification. I also attempted a textual/NLP pipeline that tokenizes incident description and removed stopwords. I found words that were specific to or most unique in time periods of 1849-1860 and 1861-1865 (Civil War). I did not have too many challenges getting the code to properly run. Historical Direction:
The data seems to support my initial hypothesis that extralegal violence differed in form across regions. In the South/Border South, 78.67% of incidents were racial/slavery related, compared to 52.65% in the West/North. This indicated that extralegal violence in the South was more tired to race and the effects of slavery. This is a decent substantial difference of over 25%, however I was expecting a percentage difference vastly bigger because the North (even pre-Civil War) had a high amount of people who were "Free Soilers". That is why I'm hypothesizing there is a high count of the word "free" in the period 1849-1860. The difference of 25% made me rethink my previous conception that the North and South had polar opposite behaviors and views on slavery. In a sense, the North were not outright about removing slavery but instead just did not want it to spread further. This was not discussed in depth in the original journal so this MVP helped me understand more nuances of the different forms of extralegal violence. I also used temporal analysis to attempt to find a shift in violence. In the earlier period (1849–1860), 65.83% of incidents were racial/slavery-related, but this drops sharply to 37.50% between 1861–1865, while “Other” violence rises to 62.50%. This suggests that the Civil War may have altered the structure or classification of extralegal violence rather than simply intensifying it. This shows that simple percentages cannot create a false illusion of violence decreasing, while in reality violence shifts thoughout time and is expressed in different forms. Finally, the NLP analysis reveals differences in how violence was described. Racial/slavery-related incidents are associated with terms such as “lynched” (57), “hanged” (54), “black” (33), and “enslaved” (29), while other incidents include words like “vigilantes” (21), “mob” (19), and “court” (19). These linguistic patterns reinforce the distinction between forms of violence, suggesting that different types of extralegal action carried different social meanings. Overall, the data supported my hypothesis that extralegal violence was regionally grouped but also revealed variations in violence across states and a significant shift over time, indicating that violence was dynamic rather static.
